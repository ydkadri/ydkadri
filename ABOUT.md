## What I Do

I lead a team of data platform engineers at Kraken Technologies. We're building systems that help utility companies access and understand their data - think 70+ million customer accounts across 30+ countries. My job is to make data easier to work with, both for analysts inside the company and for clients who need to use it.

I'm particularly focused on reducing friction. Data is only valuable if people can actually get to it and understand what it's telling them.

## How I Got Here

I've had a pretty winding career path. Started at Essence Digital working with clients like Google and eBay, where I built a self-service analytics platform that let analysts run MapReduce jobs on billion-record datasets. Building and optimizing jobs at that scale in 2012 was a serious challenge. That team went from just me to 12 people, which was wild.

After that I went self-employed for a bit, building tools for marketing tech startups. I made an awesome network graph builder for search marketing, to help small businesses figure out where to spend their ad budget.

Then I moved into consulting. First at Deloitte doing digital forensics on one of the largest investigations in history. I built a file scanner with Elasticsearch that could find documents in seconds instead of months. Then big data consulting at Informatica and Servian.

I joined Kraken in 2022 as a data engineer. I ended up leading the APAC engineering function, and now I'm the product lead for Kraken Data Sharing. We've grown from 4 engineers (down to 2 after some relocations) to 17 people across five countries. Really proud that we've had zero attrition, people seem to like working with me.

## What I'm Working On

Right now I'm focused on Kraken Data Sharing. We process operational data from the Kraken platform (it's a utilities CRM serving companies like EDF, Tokyo Gas, and Origin Energy) and make it available to both internal analysts and external clients.

The interesting technical challenge is that different clients have different data platforms. Some use Delta Sharing, others prefer Iceberg. We handle both structured data and unstructured stuff like audio files for ML use cases.

I've also built a bunch of infrastructure over the years:
- Datalake platforms and configuration tools
- Terraform modules for AWS, Databricks, and Datadog
- Data orchestration with Airflow and dbt
- Internal tools to make data pipelines easier to manage

## How I Think About Things

I think in graphs. Not charts - actual graph theory, networks, connections. I studied mathematics at King's College London (BSc + a postgrad certificate in Complex Systems Modelling), and that background has shaped how I approach problems.

I work in a relational industry - everything is databases and tables. But I discovered that when you model problems as graphs, you can solve things that are really hard or impossible in the relational world. How connections influence each other, how networks behave, how to map dependencies - that stuff is fascinating to me.

It's why I love working with Neo4j and building tools that analyze code relationships. It's also why I'm learning Rust - I want to build CLI tools that help people work with these kinds of problems.

## Leadership Philosophy

I don't do much hands-on coding anymore (though I'm starting to again with Claude's help). My job now is to enable my team to do great work.

I think about it like this: my job is to create an environment where people can creatively solve problems and take ownership of what they're building. I'm not here to micromanage or be the one with all the answers.

For client relationships, I learned in consulting that clients need to be heard, but not always listened to. Pushback is important. It's better for us to tell clients something's wrong than for them to find out themselves.

We're distributed across London, Spain, France, Tokyo, and Melbourne. It's challenging, there's no replacement for in-person time, but we make it work. My philosophy is "experiment: try things, see what works for you, throw away what doesn't quickly."

A few quotes that shape how I think about work:

**Richard Feynman**: "It doesn't matter how beautiful your theory is, it doesn't matter how smart you are. If it doesn't agree with experiment, it's wrong." This means we have to try new things, and we have to be happy getting rid of things that aren't working.

**Ted Lasso** (quoting Walt Whitman): "Be curious, not judgmental." It's very easy to judge people, especially in an async environment like Slack, but it's important to give people the benefit of the doubt and to ask questions.

**Steve Morris**: "What you see isn't what you get, what you get is what you construct." You construct your world based on the models you have built, and if you don't address the models regularly they will become obsolete.
